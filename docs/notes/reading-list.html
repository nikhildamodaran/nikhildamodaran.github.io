Economics of Growth: Thoughts on the Evolution of the Literature

Growth

  • If you look at growth literature, the citations to these papers are very high. There is a “level effect” to citation within these papers i.e. these papers are of the order of 40K magnitude while key papers in business cycle theory is 10K. The reason for this is the general and global reach of the growth literature especially since it handles big questions.

  • I also find that as one goes deep into the issue, the mass citation that earlier ideas gathered has withered away. My intuition about this is that as the literature flourishes, new strands with fresh ideas have an explosion effect. But as and when the literature matures, the new ideas are more incremental and hence the citations are more spread out. You can see this in papers by Acemoglou (Directed Technical Change) where he is pushing the endogenous growth literature in a certain direction, but is not catching up to gain momentum and is viewed as important but not explosive.

  • The recent discussion in the backdrop of thing literature is even more interesting. On one hand the discussion is getting into nitty gritty of the productivity measurement debate through the lens of misallocation. On the other hand, new aspects of this debate are making way partly due to the evolution of the economies themselves. For instance, the discussion on the role of ideas in growth and the implications of a declining population on growth are very recent and interesting.

Environment and Growth

  1. Acemoglu, D., Aghion, P., Bursztyn, L., & Hemous, D. (2012). The environment and directed technical change. American economic review, 102(1), 131-166.
  • This paper is interesting in the sense that it pushes the endogenous growth literature, which was narrowed into directed technical change, into a new direction by linking it to the environment. The paper is not explosive in the sense that it is not a new idea but it is important in the sense that it is pushing the literature in a new direction.
  1. Hassler, J., Krusell, P., & Olovsson, C. (2021). Directed technical change as a response to natural resource scarcity. Journal of Political Economy, 129(11), 3039-3072.

Mechanisms of Growth

  1. D. Acemoglu, P. Aghion, and F. Zilibotti. Distance to frontier, selection, and economic growth. Journal of the European Economic association, 4(1):37–74, 2006.
  • Growth mechanisms differ by:distance to frontier. If an economy is far from the frontier → imitation. If an economy is close to the frontier → innovation.

Institutions and Growth

  1. Acemoglu, D., Johnson, S., & Robinson, J. A. (2001). The colonial origins of comparative development: An empirical investigation. American economic review, 91(5), 1369-1401.
  • This paper is interesting because of the way it links the colonial history of a country to its current economic performance through the channel of institutions. It proposes a novel instrument for institutions and brings an interesting discussion on the role of institutions in economic development.
  1. Rodrik, D., Subramanian, A., & Trebbi, F. (2004). Institutions rule: the primacy of institutions over geography and integration in economic development. Journal of economic growth, 9(2), 131-165.

Misallocation and Growth

  • Countries are not poor because they lack capital—they are poor because they use capital inefficiently.

  1. Hsieh, C. T., & Klenow, P. J. (2009). Misallocation and manufacturing TFP in China and India. The Quarterly journal of economics, 124(4), 1403-1448.

  2. Restuccia, D., & Rogerson, R. (2008). Policy distortions and aggregate productivity with heterogeneous establishments. Review of Economic dynamics, 11(4), 707-720.

  • In an efficient economy, we have high productive firms that are large and low productive firms that are small. However, with distortions such as taxes, subsidies, regulations, and entry barriers, we have productive firms that are too small and unproductive firms that are too large. This leads to a fall in aggregate output.
  1. Bartelsman, E., Haltiwanger, J., & Scarpetta, S. (2013). Cross-country differences in productivity: The role of allocation and selection. American economic review, 103(1), 305-334.
